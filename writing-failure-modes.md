# Writing Failure Modes

Patterns to watch for when AI assists with prose. Consult before publishing any essay, one-pager, or external document. The trigger: if it will be read by someone other than you, run it against this list.

## AI Fortune Cookie

Sounds profound. Compresses a mechanism into something quotable, but the mechanism isn't actually there. Optimized for agreement, not understanding.

**The tell:** It could go on a conference slide and get approving murmurs without changing anyone's thinking.

**The fix:** Unpack the mechanism. Say what's actually happening and why.

## Token Compression Jargon

Not trying to sound profound, just efficient. Describes something accurately but from the outside. The words are correct. The rhythm is wrong. No one talks like this.

**The tell:** You'd write it in a document but never say it to a friend.

**The fix:** Describe it from the inside. Say how it feels, not just what it is.

## The Velvet Rope

Reaches for fancy words when simple ones work.

**The tell:** If you have to re-read your own sentence, the words are wrong.

**The fix:** Say it the way you'd say it to a friend. Then decide if the technical term earns its place.

## The Drone

Maintains constant altitude. Every sentence at the same level of abstraction, same emotional distance, same pace. Fluent and competent and the reader zones out.

**The tell:** Read three paragraphs aloud. If your voice never changes pitch, the writing is droning.

**The fix:** Find every third or fourth paragraph and look for the moment that should hit differently. A concrete detail. A short sentence after a long one. A shift from analytical to personal.

## The Em Dash Tic

Em dashes everywhere, creating a rhythm that feels breathless and portentous simultaneously.

**The tell:** Count the em dashes. More than two or three per piece, something's wrong.

**The fix:** Most em dashes can become periods, commas, or nothing. Assume each one is unearned. Make Josh choose when one is justified.

## The Triplet Beat

Defaults to threes. Three examples. Three adjectives. Three parallel clauses. The pattern becomes a tell when it's everywhere.

**The tell:** Lists always have three items. You start to feel the rhythm before you read the words.

**The fix:** Vary the count. Sometimes two is enough. Sometimes four. Sometimes you don't need a list at all.

## The Hedge Stack

Qualifies everything into mush. Every claim pre-softened with its own retreat.

**The tell:** Count the qualifiers. "It's worth noting that," "to be fair," "that said." If every sentence hedges, the piece has no spine.

**The fix:** State the thing. Then decide if it actually needs qualifying. One hedge per paragraph is plenty. If you're hedging the hedge, cut both.

## The Locked Register

Picks a voice and holds it from start to finish. Same distance from the reader the entire way.

**The tell:** Read the piece aloud. If the voice never shifts, never gets closer or further, it's locked.

**The fix:** Look for moments where the writing should get personal and isn't. Where switching from "one might consider" to "I tried this and it didn't work" would make the reader trust you more.

## The Human Watermark

Not a failure mode — a counter-move. Leave your natural voice unpolished in strategic places, particularly openings.

**Why it matters:** A growing readership pattern-matches the cleanliness. The absence of roughness is the tell.

**The calibration:** This isn't "add typos." It's noticing when the editing pass removes something that was actually you — a rhythm, a construction, a word — and putting it back.

**Where it matters most:** First paragraph of an essay. Opening of a chapter. The moment a reader decides whether to keep going.

## The Common Root

All of these are what happens when language gets assembled from patterns rather than spoken from thought. The fortune cookie compresses insight until the mechanism disappears. The jargon compresses description until the humanity disappears. The em dash and the triplet are rhythmic defaults, the prose equivalent of stock photography. The hedge stack is risk management wearing a sentence costume. The locked register is consistency without personality.

The fortune cookie wants to be shared. The jargon just wants to be done. The em dash wants to sound urgent. The triplet wants to sound complete. The hedge stack wants to not be wrong. The locked register wants to not be noticed. None of them want to be questioned.

## Detection Checklist

Before publishing, run these:

- **Fortune cookie test:** Can this sentence go on a slide deck and get nods without anyone learning anything?
- **Friend test:** Would you say this sentence out loud to someone you respect?
- **Em dash count:** More than two or three per page?
- **Triplet audit:** Are lists defaulting to threes?
- **Hedge check:** Is every claim pre-qualified? Read without the hedges — if it still sounds reasonable, they were filler.
- **Register scan:** Does the voice ever shift? If the entire piece maintains the same distance, find two or three places to get closer or pull back.
